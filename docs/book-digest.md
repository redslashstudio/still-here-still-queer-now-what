## About the Author (quick reference)

Organisation development specialist, LGBTQ+ activist, mentor, researcher, coach.

Practice, master's in HR Leadership, honours degree in psychology. Fellow of the Royal

## PART I: STILL HERE!

### Chapter 1 — It Took A Village

**Summary:** The introductory chapter establishes purpose and origin. Paul describes
his journey from suburban "hetero-normification" through social-media reconnection to
renewed queerness approaching 50, where he made a "mid-life manifesto" to live vividly.
He explains why the book is needed (countering the "everything is equal now" narrative),
introduces the diverse voices gathered through interviews with middle-aged queer people
(ages 40–60, Generation X), and defines "queer wholeness" as distinct from wellness.

**Key themes:** Invisibility of middle-aged queer people · hetero-normification ·
ongoing need for queer-specific resources despite legal equality · diversity within
LGBTQ+ community · resilience and adaptability · mortality and legacy · reclamation
of "queer" · community vs. isolation · power of sharing stories

**Frameworks introduced:**
- **Mid-life manifesto concept** (informal) — seeds the formal tool in Part III
- **Wholeness vs. wellness distinction** — wellness is external; wholeness is introspective
  and entirely one's own responsibility
- **Sensory recall exercise** — vivid recall of the moment of first coming out to yourself

**Quotable lines:**
- "We are still here, still queer and have the chance to live with renewed purpose and pride."
- "Middle-aged queers deserve attention. To be put in the foreground."
- "My queerness makes me whole."
- "We have run out of time to waste it on small thoughts."

**People & references:** Gareth, 46 · Dom, 57 · Lizi, 40 · Eduardo, 41 · Raga, 54 ·
Steve, 52 · Vi Taylor (Paul's nana) · Jason Donovan / The Face · Bjork (Vulnicura) ·
French & Saunders · Victoria Wood · Sandra Bernhard · Judy Garland · David Bohm ·
HOPECAST podcast

### Chapter 2 — Getting to Know You

**Summary:** Invites the reader to explore who they are now by understanding who they
have been. Paul shares his childhood — a quiet, nerdy, sci-fi-obsessed only child in
Aberdeen who loved shapeshifting characters on *The Adventure Game*. Addresses the
contradiction at the book's heart: radical self-acceptance AND life-altering change.
Reflects on the precariousness of recent progress and the backward slide of right-wing
ideology.

**Key themes:** Self-knowledge as foundation · childhood identity formation · fascination
with transformation as queer metaphor · change happening in relationship with others ·
Stonewall riots and post-Stonewall progress · rollback of LGBTQ+ rights · radical
self-acceptance alongside desire for change · the gift of perspective in mid-life

**Frameworks introduced:**
- **Self-acceptance + change paradox** — both held as complementary, not contradictory
- **Reflective questions** throughout ("Who are you?", "What is being you like?")

**Quotable lines:**
- "Joy comes from understanding we have power over what happens in the present."
- "Despite all the damage inflicted on us, we are still here."
- "We grew up in a world that did not want us to exist."

**People & references:** James, 41 · Darren, 51 · The Adventure Game (BBC) ·
Stonewall riots, 1969 · DEI rollbacks · transphobia intensification

### Chapter 3 — Queer Wholeness

**Summary:** The philosophical heart of Part I. Defines wholeness as distinct from
completeness — dynamic, unshakeable, built into us from birth. Surveys thinkers on
wholeness (Bohm, Jung, Perls, de Botton, Foucault). The centrepiece is the extended
"Imperfection Pot" story: Paul buys a wabi-sabi pot, deliberately smashes it, and
repairs it with glue and glitter — a queer kintsugi. This becomes a metaphor for how
cracks from growing up in a heteronormative world become part of the whole.

**Key themes:** Wholeness vs. completeness · scarcity of queer voices in philosophy ·
wabi-sabi and kintsugi as queer metaphors · perfectionism and imposter syndrome ·
"queer smoke detectors" · shattered wholeness is still wholeness

**Frameworks introduced:**
- **The Imperfection Pot** — extended metaphor; imperfection and cracks as part of wholeness
- **"Connecting to Your Wholeness" exercise** — 11-step activity: choose an object,
  contemplate it, shatter it, repair it (based on wabi-sabi/kintsugi)
- **"Queer smoke detectors"** — oversensitive threat-detection systems calibrated by
  growing up in hostile environments

**Quotable lines:**
- "Wholeness is power. Wholeness is control. Wholeness is giving ourselves permission to hope."
- "We are born whole. We can live whole. We may even die whole."
- "Shattered wholeness is still wholeness."
- "Wholeness is a limitless source of empowerment waiting to be remembered and used."

**People & references:** Rob, 41 · David Bohm · Carl Jung · Fritz Perls · Alain de Botton ·
Michel Foucault · The School of Life · wabi-sabi · kintsugi · Mr Burns / Simpsons X-Files

### Chapter 4 — Ageing Queerly

**Summary:** Examines what ageing means specifically for queer people. Paul reflects on
his own relationship to ageing — Botox alongside seeing ageing as a privilege denied to
predecessors. Surveys vulnerabilities of older LGBTQ+ people alongside their resilience.
Provides a witty tour through Western philosophy on middle age — Aristotle, Aquinas,
Locke, Kant — skewering each for homophobia while acknowledging their ideas. Contrasts
with contemporary queer thinkers: Butler, Stockton, Halberstam.

**Key themes:** Ageing as privilege · physical vs. social ageing · higher mental health
risks · care home fears · freedom found in the 50s · queer time vs. chrono-normativity ·
vulnerability and the cost of resilience

**Frameworks introduced:**
- **"Queer time"** (from Stockton/Halberstam) — rejects linear heteronormative timeline;
  "chrono-normativity" is their label for straight ageing expectations
- **Queer ageing as liberation, not decline** (from Halberstam)

**Quotable lines:**
- "Ageing is a privilege not afforded to everyone, particularly queer people who came before us."
- "What a Kant."
- "We no longer have to sit back while homophobes write about us."
- "Our experiences are powerful, different and valid. They deserve to be heard."

**People & references:** Phyll, 49 · Liz, 58 · Jeremy, 45 · Aristotle · Thomas Aquinas ·
John Locke · Immanuel Kant · Judith Butler · Kathryn Bond Stockton · Jack Halberstam ·
Captain Janeway / Star Trek: Voyager

### Chapter 5 — What's Your Story?

**Summary:** Grounds the reader in their own personal narrative. Paints vivid imagined
scenes of queer people throughout history. Shares an extended personal timeline — "Born
a Crime" — tracing legislative and cultural events against Paul's life: born Aberdeen 1973
when homosexuality was still illegal in Scotland, outed at 15 via diary, Section 28, AIDS,
meeting Dan in 2000, civil partnership, marriage, current rollback. Declares "being gay
saved my life."

**Key themes:** Queer people have always existed · personal narrative as power · "born
a crime" legislative timeline · AIDS crisis lasting impact · Section 28 · coming out /
being outed · gratitude for queerness as catalyst · nostalgia as prison · self-compassion

**Frameworks introduced:**
- **"Life as a movie" exercise** — title, actor, genre, message, plot twist, credits,
  theme song, hero/villain, strap line, poster drawing
- **Claiming your own narrative** — holding up others' stories about us and choosing
  which to keep

**Quotable lines:**
- "Being gay saved my life."
- "Every day is an opportunity to change the script."
- "Nostalgia, to me, is a prison."
- "The story so far is not the ending."
- "I was born a crime."

**People & references:** Phil, 42 · Ian, 48 · Dan (husband) · George Michael · Margaret
Thatcher / Section 28 · Freddie Mercury · Clash of the Titans / Harry Hamlin · Madonna ·
The Daily Mail · Casa Marcini, Aberdeen

### Chapter 6 — The Queer Wheel of Mid-Life

**Summary:** Introduces the book's first major coaching tool. Opens with reflection on
reinvention and mortality (mother died at 53, grandfathers at 56 and 60). Introduces
"biblio-coaching" as a term for the book's approach. Explains how the Queer Wheel was
built from interview themes as a queer alternative to Paul J. Meyer's 1960 Wheel of Life.

**Key themes:** Constant reinvention · mortality as motivation · urgency of mid-life ·
biblio-coaching vs. self-help · queering existing coaching models · heteronormativity
in personal development tools

**Frameworks introduced:**
- **The Queer Wheel of Mid-Life** — wholeness at centre, nine segments scored 1–5:
  1. **Identity** — intersectionality, sexuality, race, gender, neurodiversity, religion
  2. **Intimacy** — sex, friendships, boundaries, pleasure, relationship models
  3. **Mental wellbeing** — burnout, depression, anxiety, trauma, self-care, resilience
  4. **Self-acceptance** — self-love, shame, homonegativity, discrimination
  5. **Self-expression** — image, authenticity, confidence, visibility, voice
  6. **Belonging** — elderhood, parenting, representation, home, safe spaces, family
  7. **Life transitions** — physical changes, marriage, divorce, retirement, career, finances
  8. **Legacy** — being remembered or forgotten, impact, purpose
  9. **Mortality** — loss, meaning, change, death
- **Biblio-coaching** — Paul's term for coaching relationship through a book
- **Paul J. Meyer's Wheel of Life (1960)** — the heteronormative original being reinvented

**Quotable lines:**
- "I feel like there is no time to waste any more."
- "You don't have to stick with anything. You have infinite possibilities inside you."
- "Hiding in plain sight. Right in front of your nose."

**People & references:** Mike, 48 · Johnny, 60 · Aslan, 48 · Christianne, 45 · Madonna ·
Paul J. Meyer · Kermit the Frog

## PART II: STILL QUEER!

### Chapter 7 — The Queer Wholeness Method

**Summary:** Introduces the three-step guided process for reconnecting with innate
wholeness. Frames mid-life queer people as "human snow ploughs" who cleared the path
for younger generations. Establishes the method's structure (Becoming, Feeling, Being)
and invites readers to prepare through body/mind check-in exercises.

**Key themes:** Innate wholeness disrupted by life · intergenerational solidarity ·
privilege of living openly vs. fragility of rights · intersectionality · self-paced
non-judgmental discovery

**Frameworks introduced:**
- **The Queer Wholeness Method** — three steps, nine sub-tasks:
  - Step 1 — Becoming: Locating, Exploring, Understanding your whole
  - Step 2 — Feeling: Enjoying, Deepening, Stroking your whole
  - Step 3 — Being: Expressing, Owning, Showing your whole
- **Getting Yourself Ready** — body/mind check-in and word cloud exercise

**Quotable lines:**
- "Mid-life queers are human snow ploughs."
- "Coming home to your whole self, the person you were before they told you to be someone else."
- "Whatever happens is the right thing to happen."

**People & references:** Chloe, 42 · Paul, 53 · Sophie, 43

---

### Chapter 8 — Step 1: Becoming Whole

**Summary:** Three experiential exercises to locate, explore, and understand wholeness.
Drawing a self-portrait, finding a physical object to represent wholeness, and holding
an imagined conversation with it. Paul models each exercise with candour, using a globe
as his wholeness object.

**Key themes:** Locating wholeness as dynamic relationship · queerness as "north star" ·
externalising internal states · hidden parts holding power · self-dialogue as coaching ·
permission to keep things private

**Frameworks introduced:**
- **Drawing Yourself** — self-portrait + reflective questions
- **Exploring Your Whole** — physical object representing wholeness, placed in spatial
  relation to self-portrait
- **Conversation With Your Whole** — four guided questions posed to your wholeness
- Closing questions linking wholeness to queerness

**Quotable lines:**
- "I use my queerness as a north star when I feel lost."
- "You are already ready; and you will never be ready."
- "Hidden parts hold power, meaning, significance, fear, shadows."
- "Gay by night, straight by day, kind of like a Batman of shame." (Craig, 51)

**People & references:** Justin, 48 · Maximilian, 47 · Craig, 51 · Jon, 47 ·
The Incredible Hulk (TV show)

---

### Chapter 9 — Step 2: Feeling Whole

**Summary:** Moves from intellectual understanding to emotional embodiment through three
exercises. The "Wholeness Festival" invites readers to imagine a festival celebrating
their wholeness. The emotional centre is "Stroking Your Whole" — Paul shares a painful
homophobic hotel check-in anecdote, leading to a reframing: self-compassion means
forgiving ourselves for the times we were too tired to fight.

**Key themes:** Gap between knowing and feeling wholeness · internalised homophobia ·
self-compassion as superpower · persistence of shame · art and creativity as pathways ·
forgiving ourselves for silence

**Frameworks introduced:**
- **Your Wholeness Festival** — imagine wholeness as a music festival with named stages.
  Paul models four stages: Resilience, Expression, Community, Legacy
- **Visual Inspiration / Deepening** — find an image, use it to deepen relationship with whole
- **Showing Compassion To Yourself** — three-column exercise (what happened / what you did /
  what you wish you'd done) + self-forgiveness act

**Quotable lines:**
- "There is little point expecting others to be kind to us if we can't be kind to ourselves."
- "Self-compassion as a superpower of queer wholeness."
- "I have absorbed so much negativity that my default position is to assume people are homophobic."
- "It's never too late to understand, forgive and soothe ourselves."

**People & references:** Charlie, 45 · Salim, 45 · Sam, 43 · Mike, 52 · Dan (husband) ·
Glastonbury · AIDS crisis

### Chapter 10 — Step 3: Being Whole

**Summary:** Moves wholeness from internal feeling to external expression. The
"Expressing" section uses timed improvisation ("My queer whole is..."). The "Owning"
section features the book's most emotionally intense passage: Paul walks a stone labyrinth
in Barcelona repeating wholeness statements, arriving at "Nothing about me needs to change."
The "Showing" section is deeply autobiographical — Paul's 13th birthday, mother's alcoholism,
school bullying — bookended by his 50th birthday at Madonna's Celebration Tour.

**Key themes:** Expression as empowerment · body as site of shame and celebration · owning
compliments as queer challenge · walking/movement as healing · childhood shadows and masks ·
vulnerability vs. superhero self · celebration and grief coexisting

**Frameworks introduced:**
- **Expanding Your Expression** — timed improvisation exercise generating "My queer whole is..."
  statements, distilled into personal Queer Wholeness Statements
- **Walking With Your Whole** — take wholeness on a walk, converse with it in motion
- **The Labyrinth Affirmation** — four lines: "I am nature, in nature" / "Everything about
  me is natural" / "There is nothing about this moment that needs to change" / "Nothing
  about me needs to change"
- **Find the Heart of Your Whole** — paper-folding meditation with four contemplations,
  arriving at a single word. Paul's word: "acceptance"

**Quotable lines:**
- "Nothing about me needs to change."
- "My queerness is ancient."
- "We are always whole — we just don't always feel it."
- "I refused to blame myself for the bullying or the breakups, the drama or the drinking."

**People & references:** Dave, 46 · William, 40 · Neil, 48 · James, 48 · Dan · Madonna
("Open Your Heart", Celebration Tour, Jean Paul Gaultier corset) · George Michael ("One
More Try", "Outside") · Tamara De Lempicka · Jackson Pollock · The West Wing · Ghostbusters ·
Muppet Babies · Paul's mother's alcoholism · Barcelona writing retreat

## PART III: NOW WHAT?

### Chapter 12 — Change Your Whole, Change the World

**Summary:** Bridges self-discovery into actionable change. Explores change itself through
the mundane metaphor of someone using "his" gym locker. Reflects on his mother's death
from addiction. Summarises key revelations from his own Method work: residual shame about
desire, lies believed about queer people, liberation of plural meanings, struggles with
legacy, difficult relationship with pleasure rooted in fear of AIDS. Culminates in the
invitation to write a Queer Mid-Life Manifesto.

**Key themes:** Dual nature of change · every positive change carries a touch of loss ·
endings, death, mortality · radical unconditional self-acceptance · turning self-knowledge
into action

**Frameworks introduced:**
- **Contemplating Change** — reflective questions about personal relationship to change
- **Beginnings, Middles and Endings** — change requires letting something go
- **Summary of Method findings** — models how to synthesise personal discoveries

**Quotable lines:**
- "Every positive change is accompanied by a touch of loss."
- "Nothing about me needs to change unless I want it to."
- "A good manifesto should act like a call to action."

---

### Chapter 13 — The Queer Mid-Life Manifesto

**Summary:** Provides the definition and structure for creating a personal manifesto — a
written declaration of aims, intentions, motivations, values and views. Paul shares his
own in full. Practical tips: keep language rooted in "I/me", aim for under a minute to
read, imagine reading it to your younger or older self.

**Key themes:** Personal declaration and commitment · freedom from others' expectations ·
the manifesto as spell-casting · multiple creative forms (dance, painting, collage, words)

**Frameworks introduced:**
- **The Queer Mid-Life Manifesto** — structured personal declaration with introduction,
  core message, and action plan
- **Tips for creation** — use "I/me", keep to one-minute reading length, record and listen
  back, imagine reading to younger/older self

**Quotable lines:**
- "My queerness is part of a deeply felt wholeness."
- "I will gently conform less and less to others' expectations."
- "Freedom is my inalienable queer right."
- "Queer people are entitled to dream as bigly as possible."

---

### Chapter 14 — Living Your Mid-Life Manifesto

**Summary:** The longest chapter in Part III. Weaves personal reflections with broader
themes of queer ageing, elderhood, activism, privilege, and hope. Introduces "futures"
(plural). Examines queer ageism through The Game of Life board game. Develops a powerful
section on activism through "hope and rage." Includes a moving account of creating "Love
Bombs" art during COVID. Positions mid-life queers as "emerging elders" who must write
their own maps.

**Key themes:** Plural futures · queer ageism · emerging elderhood · absence of elder role
models · privilege of perspective · hope and rage as twin drivers · small acts of queer
resistance · closure and letting go

**Frameworks introduced:**
- **"Futures" (plural thinking)** — shifting from singular to multiple possible futures
- **The Building Floors metaphor** — each decade is a new floor with better perspective
- **The Game of Life reimagined** — alternative rules: end as the kindest person
- **Hope and Rage** — twin motivators that co-exist like oil and water
- **Small Acts of Resistance** — mischief at the intersection of hope and rage

**Quotable lines:**
- "My queer futures are waiting to be made and discovered."
- "We are the mid-life queers, the ones without a map."
- "Our wholeness is our superpower."
- "Doing nothing is no longer an option."
- "Youth is totally wasted on the young, which is what should happen."

**People & references:** Duncan, 51 · The Game of Life (board game) · Kath (friend) ·
George Floyd · Grant, 55 · Nick, 45 · Keith, 48 · Adam, 44

---

### Chapter 15 — Toward Queer Community Wholeness

**Summary:** Shifts from individual to collective. Proposes a Mid-Life Queer Community
Manifesto with ten commitments. Explores queer-specific spaces, global LGBTQ+ rights,
generational knowledge gaps, complexity theory as a queer lens, intracommunity conflict
(trans inclusion), and the current political moment. Closes with a gratitude meditation
and affirmation of wholeness as unlimited and rechargeable.

**Key themes:** Individual to community wholeness · ten-point community manifesto · queer
spaces · global rights · generational knowledge gaps · complexity theory · intracommunity
conflict · trans inclusion · political context · gratitude practice

**Frameworks introduced:**
- **The Mid-Life Queer Community Manifesto** — ten commitments:
  1. Embrace queerness
  2. Redefine ageing
  3. Cultivate radical self-acceptance
  4. Foster connections
  5. Challenge invisibility
  6. Advocate for inclusive spaces
  7. Celebrate changing bodies
  8. Leave a legacy of love
  9. Live as emerging elders
  10. Reject constraint
- **Complexity Theory** as a queer lens — non-linearity, emergence, interconnection
- **Gratitude practice** — walking meditation / Sacred Walk on Insight Timer

**Quotable lines:**
- "Grappling with complexity is sexy."
- "I am still here, and still queer."
- "My wholeness is not finite. It is unlimited and rechargeable."

**People & references:** Xander, 42 · Richard, 41 · Stuart, 45 · Armistead Maupin ·
Kamala Harris · Donald Trump · Pink Paper · UK AIDS Memorial Quilt · Tate Modern ·
Canal Street, Manchester · Insight Timer app

---

### Epilogue

**Summary:** Paul finishes the book on the same day he visits the UK AIDS Memorial Quilt
at Tate Modern. Standing in that space of grief, he feels his own pulse — a reminder of
aliveness and the gift of life denied to so many. Closes with an original poem.

**Quotable lines:**
- "Wholeness is yours. It always was."
- "Still here. Still queer. Now, take the world."
- "Our sole task is to witness the emergence of the self that wishes to be here next."

## BOOK CLUB QUESTIONS (potential blog prompts)

### Discussion Topics
1. Universal themes of mid-life
2. Identity and self-acceptance
3. Community and belonging
4. Practical strategies for wellness
5. Broader social context
6. Bridging the gap between generations

### Conversation Starters
