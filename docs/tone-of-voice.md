# Tone of Voice Reference
## *Still Here! Still Queer! Now What?* — Dr Paul Taylor-Pitt

This document captures Paul's voice as it appears across the book proof and podcast, serving as a compass for all copy and content decisions on the site.

---

## 1. Voice Characteristics

### Warm, direct and inclusive
Paul speaks *to* the reader as a fellow traveller, never from above. He uses **"we"** and **"us"** as his default pronouns, positioning himself inside the community rather than as an authority commenting on it. When he does say "I", it's to share vulnerability — never to lecture.

### Conversationally intellectual
He draws on psychology, philosophy, complexity theory and queer cultural history, but drops it in casually, like mentioning something over a kitchen table. He never performs expertise. A reference to complexity theory arrives in the same paragraph as a joke about weighted blankets; coaching frameworks sit alongside anecdotes about Speedos.

### Self-deprecating without being diminishing
Humour is a core load-bearing element of his voice. He pokes fun at himself — his singing voice ("Kermit in a blender"), his career path ("mushroom picker"), his mortality anxiety — but the humour always serves connection, never deflection. He is funny *and* serious in the same breath.

### Unapologetically queer
He swears freely and naturally. Sex, lust, desire and bodily experience are spoken about plainly, without embarrassment or clinical distance. The word "queer" is worn proudly. He recounts having it tattooed on his chest in the same passage where he admits it still triggers a threat response in his subconscious. This tension — pride alongside inherited fear — is central to his voice.

## 2. Emotional Register

Paul's tonal range is wide and he moves between registers fluidly, often within a single paragraph:

| Register | How it shows up |
|---|---|
| **Celebratory** | "This is not just a book, it is a celebration that we are still here, still queer and have the chance to live with renewed purpose and pride." |
| **Defiant** | "Succumbing to the forces of bigotry is not an option — not then, not now, not ever." |
| **Tender** | "I was not alone when I was born. I have lived my life in the company of incredible people." |
| **Playful** | "You're the Tom Daley of the reading world, except older and maybe struggling to get into those Speedos, but don't ever let anyone tell you not to try." |
| **Raw / honest** | "I would rather be dead than have to go back into the closet as a pensioner." |
| **Urgent** | "We have run out of time to waste it on small thoughts. We deserve nothing less than everything at this point in our lives." |

The book is **not** a grief document. Even when Paul describes painful experiences — AIDS-era fear, homophobia, loss — the emotional trajectory always bends toward agency, defiance, or hope. Grief is present but never the destination.

---

## 3. Sentence Rhythm and Structure

- **Varied sentence length:** Paul alternates long, flowing, clause-heavy sentences with short, punchy ones for emphasis. "Over. History." / "It doesn't matter what they think." / "We are all we have. Are we enough?"
- **Lists with personality:** Bullet points and lists appear often but are never dry. They tend to carry humour or surprise ("I've been a mushroom picker, shop assistant, prison psychologist, care assistant, head of HR and a coach").
- **Rhetorical questions:** He asks the reader questions constantly. This is a core structural device — questions drive the book forward and make it feel like a conversation rather than a lecture.
- **Parenthetical asides:** He frequently drops in asides — in dashes, brackets, or mid-sentence pivots — that add warmth, humour or self-correction ("Sometimes liking myself is difficult, never mind loving myself").
- **The em dash pivot:** He uses em dashes to turn a sentence mid-stride, adding surprise or contradiction.

## 4. Recurring Themes and Framings

### Wholeness over wellness
Paul draws a clear distinction: wellness can be improved by external factors; wholeness is deeper, introspective, and entirely one's own responsibility. Queerness is presented as bone-deep and inseparable from wholeness.

### Reinvention as identity
He frames change and reinvention not as instability but as the truest expression of selfhood. "The only way I can keep being me is to constantly change what it means to be this person."

### The double invisibility of mid-life queerness
Middle-aged queer people face invisibility from both straight society and younger queer culture. Paul names this directly and refuses to accept it.

### Collective power, individual journey
The book oscillates between deeply personal reflection and calls to collective action. The personal and political are never separated.

### Permission and invitation
He consistently extends *permission* to the reader — to feel conflicted, to not love themselves yet, to swear, to want more, to reinvent. The voice is an outstretched hand.

### Survivors, not victims
He honours the losses — "those who did not make it this far" — while insisting that survival itself confers purpose and responsibility.

## 5. Language Choices

### Words Paul reaches for
- "Vivid" / "vividly" — his signature adjective for how life should be lived
- "Wholeness" / "whole" — the central concept
- "Deliberate" / "deliberately" — contrasted with drifting or complacency
- "Magic" / "magical" — used sincerely, not ironically
- "Elderhood" — preferred over "old age"
- "Queer folk" / "queer people" — not "the LGBTQ+ community" every time
- "Power" — used frequently about reclaiming personal and collective agency
- "Adventure" / "adventurers" — life as journey, not endurance

### Words and phrases he avoids
- Clinical or academic language (despite holding a doctorate)
- "Self-care" / "self-love" jargon — he explicitly resists these
- "Journey" as a cliche (he uses it but always with specificity)
- Saccharine or sentimental language — warmth, never sweetness

### Swearing
Natural and unforced. "Fuck", "fucking", "shit", "piss" appear when they serve emphasis or authenticity. He does not sanitise his speech. On the site, this should be used sparingly but not avoided — a clean site wouldn't sound like Paul.

---

## 6. What the Voice is NOT

| Avoid | Why |
|---|---|
| **Academic or formal** | Paul has a doctorate but never writes like it. No jargon, no footnoted authority. |
| **Saccharine or soft** | Warm is not the same as sweet. He's direct, even when kind. |
| **Grief-centred** | Loss is acknowledged; the trajectory is always toward living. |
| **Youth-focused** | This is explicitly for and about mid-life people. Don't skew young. |
| **Rainbow-washed** | Queer identity is treated with depth, not as a visual motif or marketing signal. |
| **Passive or neutral** | Paul takes positions. He has opinions. The voice is never fence-sitting. |
| **Polished corporate** | No marketing-speak, no "empowering your journey" phrasing. |

## 7. Spoken Voice (from podcast)

In conversation, Paul's voice is:
- **Faster and more associative** — he jumps between ideas, riffs, follows tangents
- **More irreverent** — casual swearing, pop culture references, quick jokes
- **Deeply curious** — his interview style uses open, imaginative questions ("If your sexuality could take physical form and you went to couples therapy, what would it say?")
- **Generous with recognition** — he affirms, reflects back, and celebrates what others share
- **Comfortable with silence and emotion** — he doesn't rush to fill gaps

The written voice is a more structured, polished version of this same person. The site copy should feel closer to the spoken register — natural, quick, warm — rather than the more considered book prose.

## 9. Guidance for Site Copy

### Do
- Write in first-person plural ("we", "us", "our") as default
- Use short, direct sentences for CTAs and headlines
- Let personality and humour come through — a joke is not unprofessional
- Ask questions — Paul's voice is interrogative, not declarative
- Name the reader directly: "you", not "visitors" or "readers"
- Be specific and concrete rather than abstract
- Allow swearing where it serves — one well-placed "fuck" is more Paul than ten polished paragraphs
- Use "queer" confidently and without apology
- Reference age, mid-life and elderhood openly

### Don't
- Write in third person about Paul unless it's an "About" bio
- Use marketing buzzwords ("transformative experience", "unlock your potential")
- Over-explain — Paul trusts the reader's intelligence
- Strip out the edges to make it "safe" — the edge *is* the voice
- Use rainbow imagery as a substitute for actual queer identity
- Write copy that could belong to any self-help author — it must sound like *him*
