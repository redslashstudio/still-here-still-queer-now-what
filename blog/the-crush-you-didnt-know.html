The Crush You Didn't Know Was a Crush

January 2026 · From the podcast

One of the questions I ask everyone I speak to is this: do you remember the moment you first came out to yourself? Not to your parents, not to your mates, not to anyone else. To yourself.

Charlie, who is 45, couldn't pinpoint the moment. And that's the thing — for a lot of us, it wasn't a moment at all. It was a series of tiny, unconnected signals that only made sense in hindsight. A feeling you didn't have a name for. A poster you cut out of a magazine for reasons you told yourself were about the film.

For Charlie, the poster was Michael J. Fox.

Specifically: MJ in his denim jacket, the puffer vest, the chair. The full Back to the Future look. Charlie remembers keeping the magazine. Possibly cutting out the poster and putting it on his wall. At the time, the story he told himself was simple: I love Back to the Future. That's why I've done this. Because what other story was available to a kid in the late eighties? The language wasn't there. The permission wasn't there. The only framework he had was "this is a film I really like," and that had to be enough.

But it wasn't just Michael J. Fox. There was also Scott.

Scott was in Charlie's primary school class in Birmingham. Tall, olive skin, the fastest runner, the sportiest kid — the classroom jock, if primary schools had jocks. Charlie fancied him. He knows that now. At the time, he just knew that something about Scott was interesting in a way he couldn't explain. He wasn't putting two and two together. He wasn't thinking "therefore I am gay." He was just — noticing. The way queer children do, before anyone teaches them what the noticing means.

I love this about our conversations. Every single person I've spoken to has a version of this story. The poster on the wall. The boy in the class. The moment on the television that made your stomach do something you couldn't name. And every single one of us rationalised it. We were children — we were spectacularly good at rationalising. I can objectively see how someone might find him attractive. I'm just appreciating him aesthetically. The mental gymnastics of a nine-year-old who doesn't yet know the word "gay" but knows, somehow, that the thing they're feeling is supposed to be kept quiet.

What I notice, speaking to people about this now — people in their forties and fifties — is that the telling of these stories has changed. There's a tenderness to it. We can look back at our younger selves with something approaching compassion. We can see the kid with the poster on the wall and think: Oh, sweetheart. You had no idea. And we can also see the ingenuity of it. The creative storytelling. The way we made ourselves make sense in a world that hadn't made space for us yet.

That's not nothing. That's survival. And it started long before we knew we were surviving anything.
