We Are a Lost Generation

February 2026

"We are a lost generation of LGBTQ+ people. We lost so many before us." — Gareth, 46

Gareth said this about twenty minutes into our conversation and I had to pause. Not because it was new information — I knew it, we all know it — but because hearing it said so plainly felt like being winded. A lost generation. Not lost as in gone, but lost as in overlooked. Misplaced. Put down somewhere and forgotten about.

We are the generation that grew up under Section 28, which told us quite explicitly that our lives were not to be "promoted" — which in practice meant not to be mentioned, not to be visible, not to exist in any public-facing way. We are the generation that watched our elders die of AIDS while the tabloids made jokes. We are the generation that came out into a world that had no interest in hearing from us, and then — miracle of miracles — we survived long enough to see that world change. Marriage. Adoption. Legal protections. Progress.

And somewhere in that progress, we got skipped.

The culture moved on. Queer visibility exploded — and thank god it did — but it exploded around younger people. The conversations about identity, fluidity, non-binary experience, the glorious expansion of what queerness can mean: all of that is happening, and it's beautiful, and it is overwhelmingly not about us. We're too old for the youth organisations and too young for the "elderly LGBTQ+" research papers. We are, as Steve put it to me, "increasingly invisible as an older queer person." He's 52. He doesn't feel old. Neither do I. But the world has decided we've had our turn.

Here's what I keep coming back to. The generation before us — the ones who fought at Stonewall, who nursed friends through the plague years, who chain-smoked in the back rooms of pubs that no longer exist — many of them didn't make it to middle age. That's not a metaphor. They are literally not here. Which means we don't have queer elders in the way that straight people have elders. We don't have a map for this bit. Nobody left us instructions for how to be fifty and queer and still occasionally furious and also, somehow, hopeful.

So we're drawing the map ourselves. That's partly why I wrote the book, and it's entirely why I spoke to fifty-one people like Gareth — people who are navigating this stretch of life with nothing but their own nerve and each other.

We are a lost generation. But lost is not the same as gone.

We are right here. And we have a few things to say.

What would it take for you to feel seen?

Dr Paul Taylor-Pitt

Coach, mentor, LGBTQ+ activist, and author of Still Here! Still Queer! Now What? Lives in London with husband Dan and their plants, "the Sharons."
