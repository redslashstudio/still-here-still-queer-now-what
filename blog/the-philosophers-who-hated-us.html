The Philosophers Who Hated Us

February 2026

I have a confession. I spent a not insignificant portion of my doctorate reading dead philosophers. Not because I particularly wanted to, but because I was trying to find out what the Western intellectual tradition had to say about people like me growing older. About queer people in mid-life. About what it means to age when your entire existence has been treated as an aberration.

The answer, it turns out, is: nothing good.

Let's start with Aristotle — the father of Western philosophy, the man who gave us logic, ethics, and the idea that the good life is one lived in pursuit of virtue. Wonderful stuff. Truly. Except Aristotle also believed that middle age was a man's prime because by then he'd accumulated wisdom and still had physical strength. Note the word "man." Women and enslaved people didn't factor into Aristotle's golden mean. And queer people? We were, to Aristotle, the result of a biological defect. A malfunction. Not even worth philosophising about properly. Thanks, Ari.

Then there's Thomas Aquinas, who took Aristotle's framework and baptised it. Aquinas was a thirteenth-century friar who decided that sex existed solely for procreation within marriage, and anything else was a sin against nature. Homosexuality wasn't just wrong — it was, in his view, one of the gravest of all sins, worse than adultery, because at least adultery could accidentally produce a child. The intellectual gymnastics required to arrive at that conclusion are, I'll admit, almost impressive. Almost.

John Locke, hero of the Enlightenment, champion of individual liberty, wrote beautifully about the rights of man to life, liberty, and property. He also supported laws that criminalised sodomy with the death penalty. Liberty, it seems, had terms and conditions.

And then we arrive at Immanuel Kant — the great mind behind the categorical imperative, the notion that we should act only according to principles we'd want to become universal law. A reasonable-sounding idea, until Kant applies it to sex and decides that homosexual acts violate human dignity and reduce a person to an animal. Kant, the man who reportedly never travelled more than ten miles from his birthplace in Königsberg, felt qualified to make sweeping pronouncements about the full range of human desire.

What a Kant.

Now, I'm being glib. Partly because these men deserve it, and partly because I think we've spent too long treating their homophobia as a footnote rather than a feature. These are not minor blind spots. These are the foundational thinkers of Western civilisation, and they built homophobia into the architecture. When people say "it's always been this way" or "it's just natural to think of marriage as between a man and a woman" — this is where that comes from. Not from nature. From philosophy. From men in rooms, writing with absolute confidence about experiences they refused to understand.

But here's the thing that keeps me going.

We no longer have to sit back while homophobes write about us. In the last thirty years, queer thinkers have begun to build their own philosophical frameworks — not as footnotes to straight philosophy, but as something entirely new.

Judith Butler blew open the idea that gender is something we perform rather than something we are, and in doing so gave us the language to question every assumption baked into those old frameworks. Kathryn Bond Stockton introduced the idea of "growing sideways" — that queer children don't develop along the straight, linear timeline that society expects, and that this sideways growth is not a failure but a different kind of richness. And Jack Halberstam gave us the concept of "queer time" — the radical notion that we don't have to measure our lives by heteronormative milestones. No marriage by thirty, no kids by thirty-five, no midlife crisis on schedule, no retirement, no decline. We get to define our own timeline.

I find that genuinely thrilling. After centuries of being pathologised, criminalised, and philosophically dismissed, we are finally the ones doing the thinking. We are writing about ourselves, for ourselves, from the inside. Our experiences are powerful, different and valid. They deserve to be heard.

Aristotle wouldn't approve. But then, Aristotle also thought some people were born to be slaves. So I think we can safely move on.

Dr Paul Taylor-Pitt

Coach, mentor, LGBTQ+ activist, and author of Still Here! Still Queer! Now What? Lives in London with husband Dan and their plants, "the Sharons."
